Early age

Part 1: Competitive spirit from an early age

My name is Adianto Wibisono, but most people call me Adi or Anto. I was born in Medan, Indonesia, in 1997, and grew up with my younger brother. As the older sibling, I often felt the responsibility to take care of everything, yet over time I realized that he has been the one who supported me in ways I never expected.

I was raised by very strict parents who set countless rules at home. Looking back, I believe those rules shaped the discipline and resilience I carry today. From an early age, I was expected to follow household principles: speak Mandarin instead of Indonesian, wash my dishes after meals, and help my mother with chores. At the time, these rules felt restrictive, but they taught me about responsibility and perseverance.

Sports became my outlet. In elementary school, I was selected to represent Tangerang City in the O2SN provincial competition in Banten. Unfortunately, I was not allowed by my parents to join, and I had to redirect my focus to badminton instead. What began as a second option soon turned into a true passion. By the age of fourteen, I was able to defeat players several years older than me, including a seventeen year old senior.

Although competition at my club was intense and I often had limited playing time, I committed myself fully to training. That dedication brought results: across three inter-school tournaments, I earned two runner-up titles and one championship. More importantly, the experience taught me how to thrive even when opportunities seemed scarce. I learned to value patience, hard work, and the ability to turn setbacks into new directions.

Adolesence

Part 2: College life/Speak up/Vocal

In 2015, I began studying Graphic Design at Universitas Multimedia Nusantara. I quickly realized I was not the best in this field, but I pushed myself to keep up with demanding courses and a competitive environment. I chose this major partly because I wanted to avoid mathematics, but also because since childhood I had always been fascinated by design whether packaging or banners. Seeing how design abroad looked more polished than in Indonesia inspired me to create works of similar quality.

During college I continued sports, though without focus, as assignments and irregular schedules consumed my time. What I gained most from this period was perspective: I learned that talent alone was not enough, and recognition only comes through outstanding work. I also discovered the importance of adaptability shaping how I act and communicate depending on the circle I was in.

By my fifth semester, I became more vocal, especially when serving as secretary of the university’s volleyball student organization. I spoke up about unfair facilities and student welfare, and realized that I care deeply about others, not just myself.

Career

Part 3: Work Life

In 2019, during my seventh semester, I took a one semester break from college and worked as a part time graphic designer at a start up called Rentfix.com. There, I supported the team by creating promotional materials for both online and offline campaigns. After six months, I decided to return to university and finish my degree.

As the COVID-19 situation worsened in 2022, I looked for a remote opportunity and became a Creative Designer Intern at Togoparts.com, a Singapore-based company. However, I stayed for only two months due to device limitations and the realization that I was not comfortable working fully from home.

Later in 2021, I joined OtoProject, where I have been working until today. This role has been particularly meaningful because I have learned from people of different generations gaining life lessons from older colleagues and insights into trends from younger ones. At OtoProject, I serve as a supervisor, with responsibilities ranging from marketplace design, product photography, and copywriting to leading the visual team.

Goals

Part 4: level up/self reflect

I am now at a stage in life where I want full control over my thoughts and decisions. At 27, I feel ready to become a leader for my team. I am not resistant to criticism, but I value fairness. I see myself as a mirror I treat others the way they treat me. Regardless of who it is, even my own supervisor, I will speak up for myself and do what I believe is right. This mindset grew from my experiences working under leaders who were often ego driven, risk averse, and uninspiring.

My current goal is to level up my career and move into higher level leadership. I believe my critical thinking, maturity, and courage to speak up will allow me to contribute meaningfully and drive progress. At the same time, I remain open to learning, because I know that wisdom in leadership comes not only from confidence but also from the humility to keep growing.
